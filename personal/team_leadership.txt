TEAM LEADERSHIP GUIDE
======================

## What is Leadership?

  Leadership is not a title — it is a behavior.
  It is the ability to influence, inspire, and guide people
  toward a shared goal while bringing out the best in them.


======================
## Core Leadership Styles

  Style               Best Used When
  ------------------  ----------------------------------------
  Visionary           Setting direction, inspiring change
  Coaching            Developing individual team members
  Democratic          Building buy-in, gathering diverse input
  Pacesetting         High-performing teams, quick results
  Affiliative         Healing team rifts, boosting morale
  Commanding          Crisis situations, urgent decisions


======================
## Responsibilities of a Team Leader

  - Set clear goals            : Team knows what success looks like.
  - Communicate direction      : Keep everyone aligned on priorities.
  - Remove blockers            : Clear the path so the team can do their best work.
  - Give feedback regularly    : Don't save it all for performance reviews.
  - Recognize contributions    : Acknowledge effort and results publicly.
  - Develop team members       : Invest in their growth, not just output.
  - Make decisions             : Don't avoid hard calls — decide and move forward.
  - Be accountable             : Own outcomes, including failures.


======================
## How to Build Trust with Your Team

  - Be consistent              : Say what you'll do, do what you say.
  - Be transparent             : Share context, not just instructions.
  - Listen genuinely           : Make people feel heard, not just managed.
  - Admit mistakes             : Vulnerability builds trust faster than perfection.
  - Advocate for your team     : Represent their interests to leadership.
  - Protect your team          : Shield them from unnecessary noise and politics.
  - Follow through             : Never leave commitments unaddressed.


======================
## Giving Feedback (SBI Model)

  Situation  → Behavior  → Impact

  Constructive:
    "In the sprint review [S], when you presented without data [B],
    the stakeholders couldn't make a decision [I].
    Next time, include the metrics slide."

  Positive:
    "During the client call [S], when you stepped in to clarify the issue [B],
    it saved us from losing their confidence [I]. Well done."

  Rules:
    - Give feedback soon after the event.
    - Be specific, not general ("you always..." is not feedback).
    - Focus on behavior, not personality.
    - Balance recognition with correction.


======================
## Running Effective 1:1s

  Frequency    : Weekly or bi-weekly (30–45 min)
  Who owns it  : The team member — it's their meeting, not yours.

  Agenda:
    1. How are you doing? (personal check-in)
    2. What are you working on? (priorities & progress)
    3. Any blockers I can help remove?
    4. Feedback — for them and from them
    5. Career / growth discussion (monthly)

  Tips:
    - Take notes and follow up on action items.
    - Don't turn 1:1s into status updates — that's what standups are for.
    - Ask "What do you need from me?" every session.


======================
## Delegation Framework

  Step 1  : Choose the right person (skill + capacity).
  Step 2  : Define the outcome clearly, not the method.
  Step 3  : Give the context and why it matters.
  Step 4  : Agree on checkpoints — not micromanagement, but visibility.
  Step 5  : Provide the resources and authority needed.
  Step 6  : Let them work — trust the process.
  Step 7  : Give feedback after completion.

  Delegation Levels:
    Level 1 — "Do exactly this."
    Level 2 — "Research and recommend, I'll decide."
    Level 3 — "Decide and inform me after."
    Level 4 — "Own it fully — update me only if needed."


======================
## Handling Conflict in the Team

  - Address it early           : Small conflicts grow if ignored.
  - Stay neutral               : Don't take sides before hearing all perspectives.
  - Focus on the issue         : Not the person.
  - Facilitate a conversation  : Bring both parties together, not separately.
  - Find common ground         : What does each person actually need?
  - Document agreements        : Put the resolution in writing.
  - Follow up                  : Check that the resolution is holding.


======================
## Motivating Your Team

  Intrinsic Motivators (long-lasting):
    - Autonomy      : Let them own their work.
    - Mastery       : Give stretch assignments that help them grow.
    - Purpose       : Connect their work to the bigger mission.

  Extrinsic Motivators (short-term):
    - Recognition   : Public praise, shout-outs, awards.
    - Rewards       : Bonuses, promotions, perks.
    - Feedback      : People want to know how they're doing.

  What kills motivation:
    - Micromanagement
    - Unfair treatment
    - No recognition
    - No growth opportunity
    - Unclear expectations


======================
## Leadership Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

  - Micromanaging              : Destroys autonomy and trust.
  - Avoiding difficult conversations : Problems don't solve themselves.
  - Playing favorites          : Erodes team morale fast.
  - Taking credit              : Share wins with the team, own the losses.
  - Being unavailable          : Your team needs access to you.
  - Changing direction too often: Creates confusion and frustration.
  - Leading by fear            : Short-term compliance, long-term disengagement.


======================
## Golden Rules of Leadership

  1. Lead by example — your behavior sets the team's standard.
  2. Clarity is kindness — vague instructions waste everyone's time.
  3. People over process — rules serve the team, not the other way around.
  4. Develop others — your job is to make your team better, not just productive.
  5. Listen twice as much as you speak.
  6. Celebrate progress, not just results.
  7. A leader's job is to create more leaders, not more followers.
