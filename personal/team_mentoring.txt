TEAM MENTORING GUIDE
=====================

## What is Mentoring?

  Mentoring is a relationship where an experienced person (mentor)
  guides a less experienced person (mentee) to grow professionally
  and personally — through advice, feedback, experience sharing,
  and genuine investment in their success.

  Mentoring vs Managing:
    Manager   — responsible for your output and performance.
    Mentor    — invested in your growth and potential.


=====================
## Types of Mentoring

  Formal Mentoring      : Structured, assigned, goal-driven (org programs).
  Informal Mentoring    : Natural relationship built over time.
  Peer Mentoring        : Colleagues at similar levels learning from each other.
  Reverse Mentoring     : Junior mentors senior on new skills (tech, trends).
  Group Mentoring       : One mentor guides a small group.
  Situational Mentoring : Short-term, focused on a specific challenge or skill.


=====================
## The Mentor's Role

  - Guide, don't direct       : Ask questions that lead to their own answers.
  - Share experience          : Stories of your own failures and wins are powerful.
  - Give honest feedback      : Kind but truthful — not just what they want to hear.
  - Open doors                : Make introductions, share opportunities.
  - Listen first              : Understand where they are before advising.
  - Challenge their thinking  : Push them beyond their comfort zone.
  - Celebrate their growth    : Acknowledge progress, not just outcomes.
  - Respect confidentiality   : What's shared in mentoring stays there.


=====================
## The Mentee's Role

  - Come prepared             : Have questions and topics ready for each session.
  - Be open and honest        : Share struggles, not just wins.
  - Act on advice             : Mentors stay engaged when they see progress.
  - Follow through            : Do what you said you'd do before the next session.
  - Ask for what you need     : Don't wait for the mentor to guess.
  - Respect their time        : Be punctual and focused.
  - Give feedback             : Let the mentor know what is and isn't helping.


=====================
## Mentoring Session Structure

  Frequency    : Bi-weekly or monthly (45–60 min)

  Agenda:
    1. Check-in           — How are things going? (5 min)
    2. Progress Review    — Updates since last session (10 min)
    3. Main Topic         — Deep dive on the focus area (25 min)
    4. Advice & Feedback  — Mentor shares perspective (10 min)
    5. Action Items       — What will the mentee do before next session (5 min)
    6. Close              — Any final thoughts or questions (5 min)


=====================
## Powerful Mentoring Questions to Ask

  For self-awareness:
    - "What are your biggest strengths? Where do you struggle?"
    - "What does success look like for you in 2 years?"
    - "What's holding you back right now?"

  For growth:
    - "What's one thing you could do differently?"
    - "What skill, if improved, would make the biggest difference?"
    - "Who do you admire professionally and why?"

  For problem-solving:
    - "What options have you already considered?"
    - "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
    - "What advice would you give a friend in your situation?"

  For reflection:
    - "What did you learn from that experience?"
    - "What would you do differently if you could go back?"
    - "What are you most proud of this month?"


=====================
## Setting Mentoring Goals (SMART)

  S — Specific   : Clearly defined, not vague.
  M — Measurable : How will you know it's achieved?
  A — Achievable : Realistic given current skills and time.
  R — Relevant   : Tied to career or personal growth goals.
  T — Time-bound : Has a clear deadline.

  Example:
    "I will improve my public speaking by presenting in 2 team meetings
    and attending one workshop by end of Q2."


=====================
## Giving Feedback as a Mentor

  - Be specific               : "In the meeting, when you..." not "You always..."
  - Balance positive and constructive : Don't only correct — also affirm.
  - Focus on behavior         : Not personality or character.
  - Make it actionable        : Feedback without a next step is just criticism.
  - Invite their reaction     : "How does that land with you?"

  Feedback Framework (SBI):
    Situation   → Behavior   → Impact

    "When you presented the project update last week [S],
    you skipped the risks section [B],
    which left the team uncertain about the timeline [I].
    Next time, include blockers even if they're unresolved."


=====================
## Building a Strong Mentoring Relationship

  - Establish trust early     : Agree on confidentiality and openness.
  - Set expectations upfront  : Frequency, format, goals, boundaries.
  - Be consistent             : Show up prepared and on time, every time.
  - Stay curious              : Genuine interest in the mentee's world.
  - Adapt your style          : What works for one mentee may not work for another.
  - Review progress regularly : Revisit goals every quarter.
  - Know when to close        : A good mentoring relationship has a natural arc.


=====================
## Mentoring Development Areas (Common Focus Areas)

  Technical Skills    : Deepening expertise in their domain.
  Communication       : Written, verbal, and presentation skills.
  Leadership          : Taking ownership, influencing without authority.
  Problem Solving     : Structured thinking, decision-making.
  Networking          : Building professional relationships.
  Career Planning     : Mapping out short and long-term goals.
  Confidence          : Overcoming self-doubt and imposter syndrome.
  Work-Life Balance   : Managing energy, boundaries, and priorities.


=====================
## Mentoring Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

  - Giving all the answers    : Teach them to think, not what to think.
  - Making it about yourself  : The session is for the mentee, not the mentor.
  - Being inconsistent        : Cancelling often damages trust.
  - Only focusing on problems : Celebrate wins and strengths too.
  - Pushing your career path  : Guide toward their goals, not yours.
  - Skipping the check-in     : Emotional state affects everything else.
  - Ignoring follow-up        : Action items with no accountability go nowhere.


=====================
## Golden Rules of Mentoring

  1. Listen more than you advise.
  2. Ask questions before giving answers.
  3. Share your failures — they teach more than your successes.
  4. Invest in the person, not just their current role.
  5. The best mentors make themselves unnecessary over time.
  6. Growth is uncomfortable — encourage them through it.
  7. Be the mentor you wish you had.
